Sugar — what actually matters

Eat freely
Whole fruit
Fructose packaged with fiber slows absorption completely. No meaningful insulin spike. Bananas pre-training are ideal — natural sugars plus potassium.
Eat freely
Greek yoghurt and milk (lactose)
Lactose causes minimal insulin response. Comes with protein and calcium. Not the sugar to worry about at all.
Eat freely
Root vegetables
Sweet potato, carrots, beetroot — sugar packaged with fiber and micronutrients. Behaves completely differently to refined sugar.
Reduce
Fruit juice
All the sugar of fruit with none of the fiber. Same blood sugar spike as a soft drink. Eat the fruit whole instead.
Reduce
Sauces and condiments
Hidden refined sugar in ketchup, BBQ sauce, flavoured drinks. Often 30–40g per day unnoticed. Swap for olive oil, vinegar, spices.
Watch
Processed snacks at break
Most likely the primary source of refined sugar. Cereal bars marketed as healthy, biscuits, flavoured crisps. Replace with nuts, fruit, Greek yoghurt.

The rule: If the sugar arrives with fiber and protein — eat it freely. If it arrives alone — question it or replace it. The revised target is 80–100g total sugar with under 40g from refined or added sources.

Every single day: 30 min walk + 10 min morning mobility + 5 min core activation

Monday — upper body

Cable or band rows — 3 × 10–12
Neutral grip to protect elbow. Focus on scapular retraction. Rest 90 sec between sets.
Lat pulldown — 3 × 10–12
Wide or neutral grip. Builds the V-taper. Key priority at this physique stage. Squeeze at bottom of each rep.
Lateral raises — 3 × 12–15
Light weight only. Builds shoulder width which creates the athletic silhouette. Avoid if elbow is painful.
Dead bugs — 3 × 10 each side
Core finisher. Zero elbow or knee load. Slow and controlled — tempo matters more than speed.
Stomach vacuums — 3 × 30 sec
Tightens the transverse abdominis directly. Do these every morning as well. Empty lungs and draw navel to spine.

Wednesday — lower body

Leg press (shallow range) — 3 × 12
Stop at 90 degrees or where knee is comfortable. Check range with physio. Controlled descent on every rep.
Hip thrusts — 3 × 12–15
Zero knee or elbow load. Best glute builder available given the injuries. Full hip extension at the top.
Seated leg curl — 3 × 12
Hamstring isolation with minimal knee stress in the seated position. Pause briefly at full contraction.
Calf raises — 3 × 15–20
Bodyweight or with light load. Full range of motion — down through the heel. Builds lower leg proportion on a tall frame.
Plank — 3 × 30–45 sec
Core finisher. Elbows only if wrists uncomfortable. Progress duration by 5 seconds each week.

Friday — full body

Hip thrusts — 3 × 12
Small weight increase from Wednesday if comfortable. Track the progression each week.
Cable row — 3 × 10
Slightly heavier than Monday if recovery allows. Keep strict form over heavier weight always.
Leg press — 3 × 10
Same controlled range as Wednesday. Assess knee response — any swelling means back off.
Pallof press — 3 × 10 each side
Anti-rotation core work. Directly targets the waist area. Band or cable. Keep hips square throughout.
Bird dogs — 3 × 10 each side
Lower back and core stability. Slow, controlled, full extension. Zero joint load.

Daily — non-negotiable

Morning mobility — 10 min
Hip flexor stretch 60 sec each side, shoulder circles, wrist rotations. Protects the injured joints and corrects anterior pelvic tilt from sitting.
Core activation — 5 min
Dead bugs or bird dogs every single morning. Do these on the bedroom floor before getting up. No equipment, no excuses.
30 min walk
After dinner where possible. Burns fat without spiking hunger. Lowers post-meal blood sugar. Already doing this — keep it.
Stomach vacuums — 3 × 30 sec
Every morning, fasted. Anchored to brushing teeth or the shower. Most underrated tool for the waist recomposition.

Physique priority order

1st priority Shoulders and upper back
Creates the V-taper that makes the whole physique look athletic. Most visible transformation per unit of effort on a tall, lean frame.
2nd priority Core and waist tightness
Not about ab definition — about building the underlying muscle structure so the waist fat has something to sit against. Stomach vacuums and anti-rotation work.
3rd priority Glutes and posterior chain
Hip thrusts are the primary tool. Improves posture which immediately reduces the sagging appearance regardless of body fat.
4th priority Chest
Lower priority until elbow injury is resolved. Chest pressing movements load the elbow significantly. Focus the other areas first.

Habit anchors

Attach every new habit to something already automatic. The formula: After I [existing habit], I will [new habit]. Removes willpower from the equation entirely.

Morning anchors
After I turn off my alarm → I put my phone face-down and go to the window
After I get to the bathroom → I do stomach vacuums while brushing teeth
After I get on the bedroom floor → I do mobility and core before standing up
After I put the kettle on → I do shoulder and wrist circles while waiting
Study anchors
After I open my book or laptop → I set a 50 min timer before reading anything
After my timer goes off → I walk to refill my water bottle, no exceptions
After I sit at my desk → I check my screen is at eye level
After I finish a study block → I eat protein, not something sugary
Evening anchors
After I finish dinner → I put my shoes on and go for my walk immediately
After I get back from the walk → I prep tomorrow's water bottle and breakfast
After I finish evening study → I plug my phone in outside the bedroom
After I get into bed → no phone. Read or do nothing instead.

Identity shifts

Say these when the habit feels hard. The shift from "I'm trying to be healthier" to "I'm someone who moves every day" is what separates those who maintain this from those who stop.

When you want to skip the walk
"I am someone who moves every day. The walk is part of who I am, not something I do when I feel like it. Five minutes in I'll be glad I went."
When you want something sugary
"I fuel my body to perform and recover. That choice keeps me where I want to be. I can have something that actually helps me."
When you want to stay on your phone at night
"Sleep is when I actually grow. This hour of scrolling costs me tomorrow's recovery. The phone goes out."
When injuries make training feel pointless
"I train around my injuries, not through them. Every session I show up for is building the habit and the base. The physio is handling the rest."
On hard study days when everything slips
"One bad day doesn't break who I am. I get the walk in, sleep well, and tomorrow is a full reset. That's it."

School habits

6–7 hours of opportunity
School is not separate from the plan — it is part of it. The right habits during the school day turn those hours from a setback into an advantage.
☀
Pack lunch and snacks the night before
School canteen food is often high sugar, low protein. Having food prepared removes the temptation entirely. Anchor: do this after dinner, same time as prepping tomorrow's breakfast.
💧
Fill a 1L water bottle before leaving
Most teenagers are chronically dehydrated at school. Dehydration causes concentration dips and false hunger signals. Refill once during the day.
📐
Sit with posture in mind during every lesson
Back straight, feet flat on the floor. Anterior pelvic tilt from slouching pushes the lower abdomen forward and worsens the waist appearance over months.
🚶
Use every lesson transition actively
Walk at a purposeful pace between classes, take stairs, stand in the corridor. These micro-movements add up to meaningful insulin sensitivity throughout the day.
🥚
Protein-rich snack at break — not sugary
A boiled egg, handful of nuts, Greek yoghurt, or protein bar instead of biscuits. Keeps blood sugar stable and prevents the afternoon crash that derails evening study.
🍗
Protein first at lunch, carbs second
Eating protein and vegetables before carbohydrates measurably reduces the blood sugar spike from that meal. A simple ordering change with a real metabolic benefit.
🚶
10 min walk at lunch — every day
Prevents the post-lunch blood sugar crash and afternoon brain fog. Contributes to daily movement total. Dramatically improves afternoon lesson concentration.
⚡
Train after school, before evening study
Exercise improves cognitive function for 2–3 hours after. Study done post-training is genuinely more effective. This is a performance advantage, not just a fitness one.
🍳
Eat 40–50g protein within 45 min of training
The post-training window at 16 is the most effective recovery tool available. Prep the meal in advance. Muscle protein synthesis peaks in this window alongside natural growth hormone.

Supplements and protein

Tools, not shortcuts
At 16 with a clean diet, most protein needs are met from food. Supplements fill specific gaps — they do not replace real nutrition. Never use fat burners, mass gainers or pre-workout stimulants at this age.

Protein powders — verdict

Good
Whey protein isolate
Most researched, most bioavailable option available. Isolate is lower in lactose — less bloating. Use post-training if hitting 160g from food alone is difficult. One scoop ≈ 25g protein. Fine at 16.
Good
Casein protein
Slow-digesting protein from milk. Ideal before bed — releases amino acids over 6–8 hours during sleep when growth hormone is highest. Genuinely one of the best recovery tools at this age.
Good
Greek yoghurt and cottage cheese
Not a powder but contains casein naturally. 200g delivers 20g protein with probiotics that actively reduce the bloating experienced. Better than most supplements.
Fine
Plant protein — pea and rice blend
Complete amino acid profile when combined. Good if dairy causes digestive issues. Slightly higher in carbs. Fine at 16.
Caution
Mass gainers
500–1,000 calories per serving, mostly from maltodextrin — pure sugar. At 14–17% body fat in recomposition mode, this adds fat not muscle. Wrong tool entirely.
Avoid
Pre-workout stimulants
High-dose caffeine and stimulants inappropriate at 16. Natural testosterone makes them completely unnecessary. Avoid entirely.
Avoid
Fat burners and thermogenics
Can interfere with hormonal development at 16. Diet and training will achieve what these claim, safely and sustainably. Unnecessary.

Supplements genuinely worth considering

Vitamin D3 + K2 Consider
Particularly in winter or with limited sunlight. K2 directs calcium to bones. Critical for bone development still happening at 16.
Magnesium glycinate Consider
200–300mg before bed. Significantly improves sleep quality and muscle recovery. Low risk, high impact. One of the most underrated supplements available.
Omega-3 fish oil Consider
Reduces inflammation around injured joints. Anti-inflammatory effect directly supports physio treatment and speeds tissue recovery.
Creatine monohydrate Optional
Most researched performance supplement in existence. 3–5g daily. Considered safe at 16 by most sports medicine researchers. Not essential but genuinely effective. Discuss with GP or physio first.

Risk assessment

Know the limits
Growth plate stress — highest priority
At 16, growth plates are still open until approximately 18. Heavy compound lifts with spinal load — squats, deadlifts — must be avoided or kept very light. All resistance work should be machine-based or bodyweight until physio clears further progression.
Elbow injury aggravation
Any pulling or pushing movement that causes elbow pain stops immediately. Neutral grip is safer than pronated. No tricep extensions, no bicep curls, no push-ups until physio specifically approves. Elbow injuries at 16 can become chronic if pushed too early and managed poorly.
Knee injury and cycling
Cycling is low impact but incorrect saddle height stresses the knee. Saddle height should position the knee at around 25–30 degrees at full pedal extension. Downhill cycling specifically — the bracing position can overload an injured knee. Introduce gradually, report any swelling or pain to physio immediately.
Under-eating at 16
Dropping below 2,300 calories consistently can impair hormonal development, stunt height, reduce bone density and slow injury healing. The goal is recomposition, not aggressive cutting. If hunger is persistent, eat more protein and vegetables rather than restricting further.
Study stress and cortisol
Chronic academic stress raises cortisol which directly deposits fat around the midsection and impairs muscle growth. Sleep, movement breaks and morning sunlight are the primary management tools. During intense exam periods, reduce training intensity rather than eliminating it.
Overtraining at this age
Three resistance sessions per week is the ceiling with current injuries. More is not better. The nervous system at 16 fatigues faster than it appears to. Signs of overtraining — persistent tiredness, mood drops, declining strength — mean take an extra rest day, not push harder.

Timeline

This timeline assumes mild consistent effort — 3 sessions per week, diet on track, sleep prioritised. At 16, natural testosterone and growth hormone are at peak levels. The biology is working in your favour in a way it never will again.
Weeks 1–2
Bloating reduces as refined sugar drops and fiber increases. Energy becomes more stable through study sessions. Habits feel effortful but achievable. Sleep quality begins improving.

Body recomposition largely complete for this phase. Body fat tracking toward 11–13% through diet and recomposition alone. Muscle on the upper body visible at rest. Waist visibly tighter in undergarments. Habits now feel like identity.
Year 1
With mild consistent training, a realistic outcome is 4–6kg of muscle added and body fat reduced to 10–13%. Simply by showing up three times a week consistently and sleeping well. The physique at this point is significantly different to the starting photos — and this is the conservative projection.
